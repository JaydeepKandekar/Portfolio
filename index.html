RESEARCH INTERESTS
Hello again!
I have a wide range of research interests. My main focus is on Cosmology but my interest is also piqued by Gravitational Physics, Solar Physics, Exoplanets, Planet formation, star formation and Theoretical Physics with a focus on the study of cosmic structure.

Cosmic structures:
I really love studying the mysteries of space using cosmology, especially when it comes to looking at groups of galaxies called galaxy clusters. I want to figure out the basic rules that control how these big cosmic structures are formed, change over time, and move around. By studying galaxy clusters, I hope to learn more about how the universe is put together on a large scale, how invisible dark matter and regular matter interact, and the big processes that have shaped our universe. I'll use careful observations and theories to help us better understand how our universe has developed and what it's made of.

Planet Formation:
I'm really interested in learning how planets form and change over time. Researchers use a mix of planetary science, astrophysics, and computer simulations to figure out how different types of planets come to be. Researchers study how things like dust coming together and magnetic fields play a role from the very start to when planets are fully formed. By doing this, I hope that we understand how factors like the shape of the disk around a young star, turbulence, and how dust and gas interact affect planet formation. I'm also curious about how interactions between small building blocks and young planets create the different kinds of planets we see around other stars. Additionally, I'm excited to explore how a planet's early conditions might influence whether it can support life, which could help us understand more about where life might exist beyond Earth. In short, my work uses ideas, computer models, and observations to learn more about how planets are born and why they're so diverse.

Galaxies their Kinematics and Hydrodynamics:
In My recent Project with Dr Payel Das, I came across galaxy kinematics. We integrated the velocity dispersion profile and the orbit of the stars before and after the impact of the Sagittarius dwarf galaxy with the Milky Way galaxy. I studied some collisionless dynamics and used the method called kick drift kick to get the orbit of the stars. Galaxies consist of many stars and possibly even more dark matter particles. For a galaxy like our Milky Way, there are about 100 billion stars. You might expect that the strong gravitational forces between these stars drive how galaxies change over time. If this were true, we'd call it a "collisional" process because close encounters between stars would matter, even if they don't physically collide. However, that's not the case. The reality is that galaxies are "collisionless." This means that the effects we see aren't mainly caused by direct interactions or near misses between individual stars. Instead, they result from the collective behaviour of all the stars together. So, even though galaxies have a finite number of stars and other matter, these near misses and interactions don't play a significant role in shaping their evolution.
